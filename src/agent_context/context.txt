*   **Residual Vibration**: This refers to the unwanted vibration that persists in a mechanical system after a motion command has been completed, a machine has finished its primary motion, or the original external force has been removed. *   **Settling Time**: This is the time required for a system's response, such as oscillations or residual vibrations, to decay and remain within a specified small percentage (e.g., 1%, 2%, or 5%) of its final steady-state value following a motion or disturbance. It is a measure of how quickly a system stabilizes. The amount of residual vibration directly impacts the settling time; therefore, minimizing residual vibration is crucial for reducing it. Active isolation systems typically achieve shorter settling times than passive ones.

*   **Isolation Bandwidth**: This metric defines the specific range of frequencies over which the system provides effective vibration isolation. It is formally defined as the frequency range where the system's transmissibility is below a certain threshold, typically a value of one.

The choice between stack and bender actuator designs involves a fundamental trade-off between displacement (stroke) and force, which directly impacts the system's capabilities.

*   **Stack Actuators:** Constructed from multiple thin layers of piezoelectric ceramic material, stack actuators are designed for applications requiring high precision and power. They produce very high forces with small, precise displacements, typically in the micrometer range. Key characteristics include high stiffness, high resonant frequencies, and extremely fast response times (in the sub-millisecond or microsecond range), making them ideal for dynamic applications.
*   **Bender Actuators:** These actuators, such as bimorphs, consist of piezoelectric layers bonded to a flexible substrate. When a voltage is applied, the layers bend, producing a large lateral motion. This design provides a much larger stroke, often in the millimeter range, but at the cost of generating significantly less force. Compared to stack actuators, benders have lower stiffness, lower resonant frequencies, and slower response times.

### Hardware Design: Thermal Management

Heat generation is a critical factor that can degrade actuator performance, especially during high-frequency, high-voltage, or high-load operation. This self-heating is caused by dielectric and mechanical losses, as well as internal friction within the piezoceramic material. Without effective thermal management, system accuracy and stability can be compromised in several ways:

*   **Performance Degradation:** Temperature changes directly impact the piezoelectric coefficient, which can alter the actuator's displacement and cause instability. Self-heating can lead to a decrease in displacement amplitude (stroke) and a shift or drop in the actuator's resonant frequency.
*   **Permanent Damage:** Excessive temperatures can cause the piezoelectric material to depolarize, leading to a permanent degradation of its electromechanical properties and potential failure. To ensure stability and prevent damage, the operating temperature must be kept well below the material's Curie temperature.

Therefore, effective thermal management strategies, such as the use of heat sinks, are essential to maintain stable, reliable, and precise performance in demanding applications.
