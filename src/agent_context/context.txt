Search Query: "Need for Closure" psychological theory Arie Kruglanski facets measurement scale
Search Answer: The "need for closure" (NFC), or "need for cognitive closure" (NFCC), is a psychological theory describing an individual's desire for a clear, firm answer to a question in order to resolve ambiguity [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(psychology)]. This trait is measured using the Need for Closure Scale (NFCS), which was developed in 1994 by D. M. Webster and Arie W. Kruglanski to quantify an individual's level of this motivational construct [https://www.researchgate.net/publication/232502937_What_the_Need_for_Closure_Scale_Measures_and_What_It_Does_Not_Toward_Differentiating_Among_Related_Epistemic_Motives, https://nibmehub.com/opac-service/pdf/read/The%20Psychology%20of%20Closed%20Mindedness%20by%20Arie%20W.%20Kruglanski.pdf, https://www.zimbardo.com/need-for-closure-psychology-definition-history-examples/].

In 2007, researchers Roets and Van Hiel revised the original scale. This revision was prompted in part by findings that the original scale's "Decisiveness" items measured the ability to achieve cognitive closure rather than the motivation or need for it [https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0191886910004344]. The revised NFCS, which contains 41 items, has been validated as a one-dimensional measure, and an abridged version is also available for researchers [https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0191886910004344].

For individuals with a high need for closure, new information that challenges their existing beliefs can sometimes lead to mood instability. Among students, a high need for cognitive closure may even inhibit the cognitive functions necessary for learning [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(psychology)].

Search Answer: A high need for cognitive closure (NFC or NFCC), defined as the desire for a firm answer and an aversion to ambiguity, is a robust predictor of susceptibility to misinformation [1, 2, 6, 7]. Meta-analyses and systematic reviews have confirmed a significant, small-to-medium positive association between a high need for closure and the belief in misinformation, with one study reporting a correlation coefficient of r = .20 [1, 7, 8, 9, 10].

The primary mechanism explaining this link involves two cognitive tendencies: "seizing" and "freezing" [3, 9].
*   **Seizing:** Individuals with a high NFC are motivated to quickly "seize" upon information to reduce feelings of uncertainty and find an unambiguous answer [2, 6, 10]. This can lead them to accept the first piece of information they encounter with reduced scrutiny of its accuracy [1, 2, 7].
*   **Freezing:** After seizing an answer, they tend to "freeze" on that conclusion, becoming resistant to changing their minds or considering contradictory evidence [3, 10].

Research indicates that the "seizing" aspect is a stronger predictor of misinformation belief than the "freezing" aspect [10]. This overall tendency means individuals high in NFC are more likely to endorse false information and are less able to discern between true and false news [3, 4, 7]. This is linked to less analytic thinking and a "motivated lack of information processing," which are known predictors of believing false news [3, 5].

The relationship between NFC and misinformation acceptance has been observed across various contexts, including general political misinformation, COVID-19 misinformation, and conspiracy theories [4, 5, 6, 7, 8]. The effect is often stronger when the misinformation aligns with an individual's pre-existing political leanings, and NFC itself has been linked to partisan-motivated reasoning [1, 4, 5]. However, the association between NFC and belief in fake news can persist even when controlling for other factors like cognitive reflection and political ideology [1, 5]. Finally, this relationship is partially mediated by epistemic self-regulation, which refers to how people manage their own knowledge and beliefs [1].
