Warren Buffett's investment philosophy is centered on several core concepts, many of which he learned from his mentor, Benjamin Graham. These principles guide his approach to long-term value investing.

*   **Circle of Competence**: This principle dictates that investors should only invest in companies and industries they can thoroughly understand. The size of this circle is less important than knowing its boundaries. By operating within their area of deep knowledge, investors can more accurately assess a company's long-term prospects, value, and potential risks, leading to better-informed decisions.

*   **Margin of Safety**: Considered a cornerstone of value investing, the margin of safety involves purchasing a security at a price significantly below its intrinsic or underlying value. This gap between the purchase price and the company's true worth acts as a cushion against errors in judgment, unforeseen business problems, bad luck, or market volatility. Buffett has called this concept "the three most important words in investing."

*   **Economic Moat**: Popularized by Buffett, an "economic moat" is a distinct and sustainable competitive advantage that protects a company's long-term profits and market share from competitors, much like a moat protects a castle. Sources of a moat can include a strong brand identity, intangible assets like patents, high customer switching costs, or a significant cost advantage. Buffett seeks out companies with wide and enduring moats.

*   **Mr. Market**: This is an allegory created by Benjamin Graham to illustrate the stock market's irrationality. Mr. Market is a manic-depressive business partner who offers to buy your shares or sell you his every day at prices driven by his mood, which swings between euphoria and despair. The lesson is that a wise investor should not be swayed by the market's whims. Instead, they should view these fluctuations as an opportunity—buying when Mr. Market is pessimistic and prices are low, and considering selling when he is euphoric and prices are high.

*   **Owner Earnings**: Buffett considers this his preferred metric for valuation, viewing it as more accurate than net income for assessing a company's true value. It represents the actual cash flow available to a company's owners after accounting for the investments needed to maintain its competitive position and unit volume. The most common formula is: Net Income + Depreciation/Amortization - Capital Expenditures. More detailed variations may also account for changes in working capital or use the average annual maintenance capital expenditure.

*   **Return on Equity (ROE)**: He seeks companies that demonstrate a consistently high ROE, as this indicates how effectively management is using shareholder equity to generate profits. The specific benchmark is an ROE that has been consistently at or above 15% annually for the past ten years. A high and sustainable ROE is often the result of a durable competitive advantage, but Buffett also ensures this high return is not achieved through the use of excessive debt.

*   **Debt-to-Equity (D/E) Ratio**: A low level of debt is a critical criterion. Buffett prefers companies with little to no debt that do not rely on borrowed money to generate profits. A common quantitative guideline is to look for a debt-to-equity ratio below 0.5 or to ensure that a company's total debt is less than 50% of its total capital.

*   **Consistent Profitability**: Buffett looks for a long-term history of consistent, stable, and predictable earnings, avoiding companies with volatile profits. He often examines up to ten years of financial statements to find a clear, upward trend in Earnings Per Share (EPS). High profit margins are also considered a key indicator of a strong and durable business.

### Duan Yongping's Investment Philosophy

Duan Yongping's investment philosophy is rooted in determining a company's intrinsic value, which he defines as "the sum of the present value of all future cash flows of a business." This is the only definition he considers valid. His approach is a qualitative thought process rather than a precise, formulaic calculation.

**Valuation Approach: Roughly Right, Not Precisely Wrong**

Duan uses a Discounted Cash Flow (DCF) model mentally, stating, "I basically use the DCF (Discounted Cash Flow) model in my mind." He emphasizes being "roughly right" instead of "precisely wrong," cautioning against an over-reliance on complex models and spreadsheets, which he does not use for valuation. For him, a company's intrinsic value is a "rough range," not a precise number. This intuitive process is built on a deep understanding of the business, which he compares to recognizing a beautiful person without needing to measure their specific features.

**The 10-Year Horizon**

A cornerstone of his philosophy is a ten-year investment horizon. A critical prerequisite for any investment is the ability to understand the business well enough to project what it will look like in a decade. As he puts it, "if I can’t see what it will be like in 10 years, I won’t buy it." If he cannot roughly calculate a company's value or envision its future state, he will not invest.

**Determining a "Reasonable Price"**

Duan's methods for identifying a reasonable purchase price are practical and tied to his long-term outlook:

*   **The "Whole Company" Test**: He poses a simple question: "If I have the money, should I buy this whole company with this price?" If the answer is yes, he considers the price to be cheap.
*   **Payback Period**: He defines a good price as one that allows him to make his money back within a reasonable timeframe, typically 8 to 10 years. A payback period of 20 to 30 years would require much more careful consideration. The goal is to achieve a "satisfactory return" over ten years, using the long-term treasury yield as a potential benchmark.
*   **Margin of Safety**: He seeks a significant margin of safety, sometimes aiming to purchase a company at a 50% discount to his calculated intrinsic value.

**View on P/E Ratio and Final Considerations**

Duan uses the Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio only as a quick reference after he has already developed a deep understanding of the business. He frames it as a payback period: "A PE of 10x means that I can get my money back in 10 years... Is that a good deal? You have to make your own judgment call." Ultimately, the price is the last factor he considers, after conducting a thorough analysis of the company's business model and management.
